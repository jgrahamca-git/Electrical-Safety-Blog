### 1. What is SCCR?

The Short-Circuit Current Rating (SCCR) is the maximum level of short-circuit current that an electrical component or an entire control panel can safely withstand without catastrophic failure (i.e., without bursting into flames, expelling shrapnel, or creating a shock hazard).

It is a critical safety parameter mandated by NEC 110.10 and UL 508A Supplement SB in the US, and by CEC Rule 2-024 (interrupting capacity) and CSA C22.2 No. 286 (Industrial Control Panels and Assemblies) in Canada. The SCCR of a panel must be greater than or equal to the available fault current at the terminals where the panel is installed. If the available fault current exceeds the SCCR, a short circuit inside the panel could cause an explosion.

### 2. The "Weakest Link" Principle

The most important and often misunderstood rule of calculating a panel's overall SCCR is the "weakest link" principle. A control panel is only as strong as its lowest-rated component in the primary power circuit.

If you build a panel using a main disconnect rated for 100kA, a contactor rated for 65kA, but you install a small power distribution block that is only rated for 5kA, the entire panel's SCCR drops to 5kA. Installing that panel in a location with 20kA of available fault current is a massive code violation and a lethal safety hazard.

### 3. The Hazard of Modifications

Many panels leave the original equipment manufacturer (OEM) with a perfectly acceptable 65kA SCCR label. However, over the lifespan of the equipment, maintenance technicians inevitably make modifications.

A technician might replace a failed variable frequency drive (VFD) with a different brand, or swap out a set of fuses for a breaker to save time on replacements. If the new component has a lower standalone SCCR, or if a specific breaker-contactor series rating is broken, the entire panel's SCCR is instantly compromised. The label on the door becomes a lie.

### 4. Actionable Takeaways

- **Verify Available Fault Current:** Before installing any new control panel, you must calculate the available fault current at that specific node in the facility's power system and ensure the panel's SCCR is higher.
- **MOC for Replacements:** Any replacement of a power component (breaker, fuse, contactor, VFD, distribution block) must go through a Management of Change (MOC) process to verify it does not lower the panel's overall SCCR.
- **Use Series Ratings Carefully:** Sometimes a low-rated component can be used if it is protected by a specific, tested current-limiting fuse or breaker upstream. However, this "series rating" is highly specific to exact part numbers and cannot be substituted.
